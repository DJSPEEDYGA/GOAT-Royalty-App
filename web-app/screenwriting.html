📐 The Three-Act Structure — Screenplay Blueprint

The foundational framework used by 90% of Hollywood films — from 1-page shorts to 180-page epics

Act I — Setup (Pages 1–30)

~25% of screenplay • Establish world, characters, stakes

Introduce your protagonist in their ordinary world. Establish the tone, genre, and central dramatic question. End with the Inciting Incident — the event that disrupts equilibrium and forces the hero into action.

🔥 Plot Point 1 — The Break Into Act II (Page ~25-30)

The "point of no return" — the protagonist commits to the journey

This is the first major turning point. The protagonist makes a choice (or is forced into one) that launches the central conflict. There's no going back to the way things were. Examples: Luke leaves Tatooine. Neo takes the red pill.

Act II — Confrontation (Pages 30–90)

~50% of screenplay • Rising action, obstacles, complications

The longest act. The protagonist pursues their goal but faces escalating obstacles. Subplots develop. Allies and enemies are revealed. The Midpoint (page ~55-60) is a major twist that raises stakes and shifts direction — often a false victory or false defeat.

💀 All Is Lost Moment (Pages ~75-85)

The "dark night of the soul" — lowest point for the protagonist

Everything seems hopeless. The protagonist has lost everything they've gained. This is the emotional low point that forces them to look inward and find the strength or truth needed for the final push.

🔥 Plot Point 2 — The Break Into Act III (Page ~85-90)

New information or decision that propels the climax

A revelation, decision, or event gives the protagonist what they need to face the final challenge. The story accelerates toward the climax with renewed purpose and higher stakes than ever.

Act III — Resolution (Pages 90–120)

~25% of screenplay • Climax, resolution, new equilibrium

The Climax is the final confrontation — the protagonist faces their greatest challenge. All threads converge. After the climax, the Denouement shows the new world order and the transformation of the protagonist. Leave the audience satisfied (or provoked).

💡 Screenwriting Pro Tips

Wisdom from the masters — distilled into actionable advice

📖

Read Scripts Daily

Download produced screenplays from IMSDB, Script Slug, or The Black List and read at least one per week. Study structure, not just dialogue.
🎯

Write the Logline First

If you can't describe your movie in 1-2 sentences, you don't know your story yet. The logline is your compass.
⏱️

1 Page ≈ 1 Minute

Industry standard: each properly formatted screenplay page equals about one minute of screen time. A feature film = 90-120 pages.
🗣️

Read Dialogue Aloud

If it doesn't sound natural when spoken, it won't work on screen. Every character should have a distinct voice.
✂️

Enter Late, Leave Early

Start each scene as late as possible, end it as early as possible. Cut the fat. Every scene must either advance plot or reveal character.
🔄

Rewrite is Writing

The first draft is just getting the story out. The real writing happens in rewrites. Most produced scripts go through 10-20 drafts.
